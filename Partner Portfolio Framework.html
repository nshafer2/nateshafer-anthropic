Working Note / v1 Nathan Shafer · for Anthropic

An empirical approach to partner selection.

Most partner programs run on narrative. A leader says "Accenture is our strongest partner." Nobody asks what would have to be true for that to be wrong. The portfolio gets built on instinct, defended in slides, and reviewed annually.

This is a different way to do it. Score every partner on the same dimensions. State the thesis behind every managed relationship. Measure against it. Change your mind when the data says to.

The frontier moves too fast for narrative to keep up.

01 · Taxonomy

Six partner types. Six different motions.

The first mistake is treating the ecosystem as tier 1, tier 2, tier 3. That flattens the most important question, which is what kind of work each partner actually does.

McKinsey is not a smaller Accenture. A PE-backed AI-native firm is not a junior Big Four. They run different motions, get measured differently, and need different things from the vendor. Six categories. Six engagement models.

01 / GSI

Global Systems Integrators

Accenture · Capgemini · Infosys · TCS · Wipro

Massive bench. Deep delivery muscle. Transformation-program oriented. Claude lands inside multi-year enterprise programs already in flight.

Motion · Delivery interlock

02 / Big Four

Professional Services

Deloitte · PwC · EY · KPMG

Hybrid firms. Audit and risk relationships GSIs don't have. They own the C-suite seat in regulated industries. Natural home for the responsible-deployment story.

Motion · Regulated industry play

03 / Strategy

Strategy Consultancies

McKinsey · BCG · Bain · QuantumBlack · BCG X

Different motion entirely. They shape the strategy that drives the delivery spend. Influence at the C-suite, not delivery at scale. Their decks decide which model shows up later.

Motion · Executive alliance

04 / PE-Backed

AI-Native Firms

The new wave · scaled boutiques · capital-backed challengers

Built for this moment. No legacy practice politics. Capital to invest in bench ahead of revenue. Hungry. They move fast and they churn fast. Some of them will become tier 1 partners in 18 months.

Motion · Growth partnership

05 / Boutique / ISV

Specialty Firms

Technical specialists · vertical experts · ISVs building on Claude

Smaller firms with sharp focus. ISVs are different — their growth is your growth, automatically. The boutiques compound when they pick a lane and stay in it.

Motion · Embedded / product-led

06 / Specialist

Regional & Vertical

Country, industry, workflow specialists

Own a lane. Smaller in absolute terms but defensible. Critical for industry coverage across the managed book — the place specialization actually lives.

Motion · Lane defense

02 · Portfolio Positioning

Most partner programs score on readiness. That misses half the picture.

A partner that's eager but irrelevant doesn't move the needle. A partner that's relevant but stuck does — once you unlock them. Score every partner on two axes, not one. Strategic value. Velocity of Claude adoption.

The quadrant below isn't a snapshot. It's a map of where attention goes. Anchor bets get half the team's hours. Optionality gets the systems and nothing else. The bet on Series-A-equivalent partners is that some of them become anchors faster than the incumbents.

Velocity beats current state. A partner growing 4x off a smaller base is a better bet than one stagnant at a bigger base.

Anchor Bets
10% of partners · 50% of attention

Pick three to five. Not twenty. Proactive engagement, joint planning, co-investment dollars, executive interlock. If a partner is in this tier and not moving, the question is whether they belong here.

Growth Bets
30% of partners · 30% of attention

Proven motion, needs fuel. Co-investment makes the biggest delta. The path to anchor runs through here.

Venture Bets
30% of partners · 15% of attention

Bet on slope, not intercept. PE-backed AI-natives. Expect 30% to wash out. The breakouts are worth the rest.

Strategic Bets
20% of partners · 5% of attention

Different return profile entirely. Measure on whether Claude shows up in their client recommendations. Don't measure on consumption. They're not delivering it.

Optionality
10% of partners · near-zero attention

Self-service. Programmatic. Most will not graduate. The one that does is worth more than the other nineteen.

03 · Dimensions

What you actually score, and how you know.

Strategic value and velocity are useful axes. They need underlying signals or they collapse into intuition. Five dimensions. Every partner sits somewhere on each. The placement on the quadrant is the output of these scores, not a separate judgment.

i.

Stage

Where they sit on the Claude adoption curve. Seed → Series A → Growth → Anchor.

→ committed practice
→ named leader
→ customer references
→ marketplace presence
ii.

Velocity

Rate of change. The best leading indicator of where a partner will be in 18 months.

→ architect growth QoQ
→ pipeline expansion
→ time-to-first-deal trend
iii.

Market Position

Where they win. Vertical, geography, workflow, executive trust, scale. Generalists don't compound.

→ industry depth
→ regional ownership
→ specialization commitment
iv.

Capital Efficiency

Customer outcome per dollar of Anthropic investment. The bet that looks small can be the highest-return one in the portfolio.

→ customer ARR per PSM hour
→ co-invest ROI
→ partner self-funding ratio
v.

Portfolio Fit

Does this bet fill a gap or duplicate one? Two GSIs in healthcare is redundant. Diversification is a design decision.

→ vertical coverage
→ geo coverage
→ capability coverage
04 · Risk

In a usage-based business, the risk is not financial.

The vendor barely takes commercial risk. The partner takes the delivery risk. The customer takes the consumption risk. What's left for the program to worry about is whether the work gets done well — and what happens when it doesn't.

That's where Anthropic's safety thesis has to land inside the partner program. Not as a slide. As a scoring dimension. Five risks. The ones that actually matter.

05 · Empirical Measurement

Run the program the way Anthropic runs the research.

Anthropic's approach to safety is empirical. Hypotheses tested against frontier systems. Measurement over prediction. Calibrated confidence over confident assertion. The partner program should run the same way.

Every managed partner gets an explicit thesis. "We believe this partner will produce X certified architects, Y in customer ARR, Z reference engagements in 12 months because of their commitment to vertical W." Then you measure against it. When the data says the thesis is wrong, you change the thesis. Not the data.

The hardest metric is the most important one. Would the customer have adopted Claude without the partner?

Leading indicators

Weekly · monthly
  • Certified architects added per quarter
  • Joint pipeline created
  • Partner-sourced customer Claude trials
  • Bench training hours invested by partner

Adoption indicators

Monthly · quarterly
  • Customer Claude consumption in joint accounts
  • Time-to-first-deal
  • Retention at 6 and 12 months
  • Expansion rate within joint accounts

Health indicators

Continuous · canaries
  • Partner-led customer escalations
  • Safety incidents and near-misses
  • Architect attrition off Claude work
  • Customer satisfaction in partner-led engagements

Counterfactual (the honest ones)

The hardest · the most important
  • Would this customer have adopted Claude without the partner?
  • Is the partner sourcing net-new, or rebadging direct?
  • What's the partner-attributable growth net of cannibalization?
  • What did we learn this quarter that changes the thesis?
06 · The Framework Stack

Four layers. Each one has a job.

Every partner sits at an intersection of these four layers. Every intersection has a playbook. The discipline is being explicit about which intersection a partner occupies — and changing the engagement model when they move.
